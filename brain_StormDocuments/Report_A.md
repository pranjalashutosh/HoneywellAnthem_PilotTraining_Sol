# FAA frameworks, industry practices, and the AI frontier in avionics transition training

**The FAA's regulatory architecture for avionics transition training — built around Flight Standardization Board reports, five-tier differences training levels, and deterministic simulator qualification standards — provides a mature but fundamentally backward-looking framework that has no provisions for AI-driven or adaptive training technologies.** This gap matters because industry players like FlightSafety International and CAE are already deploying AI-powered performance analysis and mixed-reality training tools commercially, while Honeywell's next-generation Anthem cockpit promises a paradigm shift from legacy Primus EPIC that will demand entirely new training approaches. The FAA AI Roadmap (Version I, July 2024) addresses aircraft certification exclusively and is silent on training applications. No NPRM, ARC, or ARAC working group currently addresses AI in pilot training. EASA, by contrast, published its first AI regulatory proposal (NPA 2025-07) in November 2025 and is moving toward a comprehensive framework that includes human-AI teaming. The most promising FAA regulatory pathway for AI integration is the Advanced Qualification Program (Part 121 Subpart Y), which explicitly encourages innovation in training methods and technology — but AQP is unavailable to Part 91 and Part 135 business aviation operators.

## The regulatory architecture governing avionics differences training

The FAA's framework for avionics transition training rests on three pillars: Flight Standardization Board evaluations, differences training level classifications, and FSTD qualification standards under Part 60.

**Advisory Circular 120-53B** (now cancelled, with AC 120-53C in draft) established the foundational five-level system for classifying training differences between aircraft variants. Level A requires only self-instruction through manuals and bulletins. Level B involves aided instruction such as CBT or classroom lectures. Level C mandates systems integration training using a cockpit procedures trainer with accurate system replication. **Level D requires full-task differences training** in a high-fidelity device (Level 6+ FTD) integrating knowledge, skills, and abilities in simulated flight. Level E triggers full transition training equivalent to an initial type rating, requiring a Level C or D Full Flight Simulator. The critical threshold: **aircraft with Level E training differences receive separate type ratings**, while Levels A–D remain within the existing type rating.

The FSB process determines these levels. The FSB Chairperson — an FAA Aircraft Evaluation Division operations specialist — convenes a board including operations inspectors, National Simulator Team personnel, and technical advisors to evaluate manufactured or modified aircraft. The board conducts standardized tests: T1 for functional equivalence, T2 for differences training evaluation, T3 for training course validation, and T5 for ATP practical test standards. FSB reports contain **recommended minimum training requirements** that principal operations inspectors use to approve operator training programs.

**Master Differences Requirements (MDR)** are formulated by the FSB for each related aircraft family, specifying minimum difference levels between base aircraft and variants. **Operator Differences Requirements (ODR)** are operator-specific requirements developed from MDR minimums, tailored to individual fleet compositions. New MDRs are triggered by new aircraft variants, significant avionics modifications (including STC installations), engine changes, or systems modifications affecting crew knowledge, skills, or abilities. ODR tables proposed by operators must be coordinated with the FSB prior to FAA approval to ensure consistency across operators.

Real-world FSB examples illuminate how avionics transitions are handled. The **Gulfstream GIV-X/GV/GV-SP FSB Report (Rev. 15)** documented the transition from GV to GV-SP as involving "the addition of a Honeywell Primus Epic avionics suite, which consists of four multifunction, 14-inch flat panel LCD units" — a major avionics architecture change handled within the existing type rating. The **Bombardier LR-60/LR-60XR FSB** classified the Pro Line 4 to Pro Line 21 avionics swap at Level C, adding four hours of systems integration training beyond the manufacturer's proposal. By contrast, the **Gulfstream GVI (G650) PlaneView II Block Point I** software upgrade required only Level B training with no checking or currency requirements.

## Part 142 training centers and the FSTD qualification ecosystem

**14 CFR Part 142** governs aviation training centers authorized for type ratings, proficiency checks, and transition training. Section 142.59 requires each FSTD to be "specifically qualified and approved by the Administrator" for each maneuver, procedure, make/model/series, and curriculum. Part 142 centers receive Training Specifications (TSpecs) authorizing specific curricula, with an FAA Training Center Program Manager overseeing each center. The most recent amendment, **Amendment 142-11 (89 FR 92488, November 21, 2024)**, updated organizational nomenclature.

The **National Simulator Program (NSP)** qualifies FSTDs under **14 CFR Part 60**, which establishes Full Flight Simulator levels A through D and Flight Training Device levels 4 through 7. Section 60.13 requires FSTD validation data to include "the aircraft manufacturer's flight test data and all relevant data developed after the type certificate was issued" — a fundamentally deterministic framework. When aircraft receive avionics upgrades, corresponding simulator modifications require NSP re-qualification per §60.23, including updated Master Qualification Test Guides. Recent amendments include **Amendment 60-7 (87 FR 75711, December 2022)** updating qualification procedures and **Amendment 60-8 (89 FR 92483, November 2024)**.

**A critical regulatory gap exists for Part 91 operators.** Pure Part 91 operations (non-fractional) have no mandatory requirements for avionics-specific transition training beyond the type rating itself. A Part 91 operator could theoretically install a completely new avionics suite via STC without any formal pilot transition training requirement, as long as the type rating remains unchanged. Industry best practices, NBAA guidelines, manufacturer recommendations, and insurance requirements partially fill this gap. Part 135 operators must comply with FSB report minimums under §135.293 and §135.324. Part 91K fractional ownership programs operate under §91.1065 with Management Specifications analogous to Part 135 OpSpecs.

The **Air Carrier Training ARC Recommendation 21-10** proposed revising training difference level definitions in AC 120-53, suggesting Level D training devices could range "from a flat panel trainer capable of full task difference training with a full crew up to qualified FTD Level 6 or 7" — removing the mandatory Level 6 FTD minimum and potentially expanding the role of lower-cost training devices for avionics transitions.

## How the industry currently trains pilots on new avionics

The business aviation training ecosystem operates through a layered architecture: OEM-provided knowledge training (CBT, eLearning), part-task trainers for procedures familiarization, and Level D Full Flight Simulators for integrated skills training and regulatory credit.

**FlightSafety International** operates the world's largest fleet of advanced full-flight simulators across six continents, delivering **1.4 million training hours annually**. Their technology portfolio now includes three significant innovations. **FlightSmart**, developed with IBM, monitors over 4,000 variables in real-time during simulator sessions, using AI and ML to detect latent behaviors invisible to human instructors and create personalized corrective action training paths. **MissionFit** is a mobile, reconfigurable FTD with seamless glass touchscreen displays designed for FAA Level 4/5 qualification, controllable via smartphone or tablet. **VITAL MR** mixed-reality technology uses Varjo headsets with NVG emulation, integrated avionics, and tactile controls — pilots see and interact with physical cockpit elements while immersed in virtual environments. Frasca (an FSI subsidiary) achieved EASA qualification for a NOVASIM MR DA42 trainer in Switzerland in 2025.

**CAE** operates SimuFlite at Dallas/Fort Worth — the largest business aviation training facility in the world at **426,000 square feet with 34 simulators**. CAE unveiled an Apple Vision Pro mixed-reality training application at NBAA-BACE 2024 for the Bombardier Global 7500, leveraging spatial computing for immersive cockpit familiarization. In November 2025, CAE announced rollout of Competency-Based Training and Assessment (CBTA) for business aviation recurrent training under EASA, beginning January 2026. CAE's Adaptive Learning Engine uses AI/ML for rule-based performance measurement across military and civil platforms.

**Garmin** provides direct avionics-focused training through eLearning courses (G3000 Essentials, G5000 Essentials, Aviation Weather Radar), instructor-led classroom sessions at Olathe, Kansas, and virtual courses — but does not operate FFS or qualified FTDs. Garmin's training focuses on avionics familiarization without regulatory credit; full type training occurs at FlightSafety, CAE, or SimCom simulators incorporating Garmin avionics. **Collins Aerospace** offers the Virtual Avionics Procedure Trainer (VAPT), which uses actual avionics software (not simulated graphics) matching real-world fidelity for Pro Line 21, Pro Line Fusion, and Flight2 systems. Collins also provides a portable VR trainer for Head-up Guidance System proficiency. **Honeywell** partners exclusively with FlightSafety for maintenance training and provides limited PC-based pilot training software for Primus EPIC. Pilot training on Epic-equipped aircraft is embedded in OEM-specific type rating courses at FlightSafety and CAE.

A notable gap persists: **dedicated avionics-only transition training with regulatory credit is poorly standardized.** Most FAA-credited training is aircraft-type-focused rather than avionics-suite-focused. When pilots need to transition between avionics generations on the same aircraft type, the training pathway often relies on Level A–C differences training without dedicated part-task trainer time on the new avionics.

## Honeywell Anthem represents a generational leap — but remains pre-certification

Honeywell Anthem is a clean-sheet, cloud-native integrated flight deck — the first new core avionics architecture from Honeywell in over two decades. Unveiled in October 2021 and completing its first fully managed flight on May 12, 2023, aboard Honeywell's Pilatus PC-12 testbed over Phoenix, **Anthem is not yet FAA certified as of March 2026**, with the original ~2025 aftermarket target having slipped.

The architectural differences from Primus EPIC are profound. Anthem moves processing into "system on chip" smart displays (versus Epic's modular virtual backplane network), adopts a touch-first interface eliminating traditional buttons, knobs, and mode selection panels, and introduces always-on cloud connectivity through the Honeywell Forge platform. Pilot-customizable display layouts replace fixed OEM-defined arrangements. The Integrated Network Server Unit (INSU) maintains connectivity via SATCOM, cellular, Wi-Fi, and Bluetooth — even when the aircraft is parked and powered down. **PilotPredict**, an AI-driven smart scratchpad, anticipates pilot inputs from partial entries. The Connected Mission Manager proactively pushes relevant information by flight phase rather than requiring pilots to pull it. Honeywell claims the system reduces preflight time by approximately 45 minutes through remote flight plan upload.

**No production business jet currently uses Anthem.** Confirmed platform selections are limited to Lilium Jet (eVTOL), Vertical Aerospace VX4 (eVTOL), and Boom Overture (supersonic concept) — none yet certified. Critically, **Gulfstream is not adopting Anthem** for any current or announced aircraft; the G400 through G800 use Epic-based Symmetry, and the newly announced G300 uses Honeywell Epic 2X under the "Gulfstream Harmony" flight deck. The most significant business aviation signal came in **December 2024, when Bombardier signed a strategic agreement worth up to $17 billion** that includes "collaborative research and development centered on Honeywell Anthem avionics" for future aircraft.

Honeywell is pursuing a deliberate bridging strategy. **Primus Epic 3.0**, launching in 2026, incorporates Anthem-like features — touch-enabled controls, enhanced 3D graphics, cloud-native services, and SAL-3 compliant cybersecurity — into the existing Epic architecture. Jorge Verduzco, Honeywell Product Line Director, stated: "We are intentionally blurring the lines between Epic and Anthem to enable fleet commonality and ensure a seamless transition for customers." No FSB activity, qualified FTD, or formal transition training syllabus exists for Anthem, as no certificated aircraft yet incorporates the system.

## Seven regulatory gaps block AI-driven training

The current FAA regulatory framework contains seven specific gaps that prevent AI-driven or adaptive training technologies from receiving formal credit.

**Gap 1: Part 60's deterministic paradigm.** FSTD qualification under Part 60 mandates reproducible, validated simulation behavior tested against aircraft-specific flight data through the Master Qualification Test Guide. AI-generated dynamic scenarios that vary from session to session present a **fundamental conflict** with this paradigm. No provision exists for qualifying an FSTD whose training scenarios adapt based on trainee performance.

**Gap 2: Part 142's static curriculum model.** Section 142.59 requires FAA approval for each specific curriculum and maneuver. No mechanism exists for curricula that adapt dynamically to individual trainee performance. An AI system that adjusts scenario difficulty, introduces targeted failures based on detected competency gaps, or modifies ATC communications in real-time has no pathway to curriculum approval.

**Gap 3: No standards for AI assessment tools.** Products like FlightSafety's FlightSmart and CAE's Adaptive Learning Engine operate commercially but have no regulatory framework for formal credit. No advisory circular or regulation addresses AI-based pilot performance evaluation in credited training.

**Gap 4: No framework for AI-generated ATC in training.** Part 60 Appendix A references ATC environment fidelity but assumes scripted or instructor-generated communications. Consumer products like BeyondATC (which integrated a custom LLM for natural language ATC in February 2025) and SayIntentions.AI demonstrate technical feasibility, but no standard exists for qualifying AI-driven natural language ATC systems in FSTDs.

**Gap 5: The AI Roadmap's training silence.** The FAA's "Roadmap for Artificial Intelligence Safety Assurance, Version I" (July 2024), signed by Associate Administrator David H. Boulter, focuses exclusively on aircraft certification. It explicitly states it does not address societal aspects of AI and focuses on "aircraft and related systems for inflight operations." Training, simulation, and instructional technology are entirely absent.

**Gap 6: No transition pathway from supplemental to credited AI training.** AI tools can only serve as supplemental (non-credited) training. No graduated approval process exists — unlike the AQP, which was introduced via SFAR No. 58 before permanent codification as Part 121 Subpart Y in 2005, providing a regulatory innovation model.

**Gap 7: Business aviation exclusion from AQP.** The Advanced Qualification Program, codified in Part 121 Subpart Y, is the most promising existing framework because it explicitly "encourages innovation in the methods and technology used during instruction and evaluation" (AC 120-54A), uses data-driven quality control, and supports proficiency-based rather than hours-based training. However, **AQP is available only to Part 121 operators**, excluding the Part 91 and Part 135 business aviation operators who would be primary customers for Anthem transition training.

## The 10-year horizon for AI-driven avionics training

The convergence of three forces — ICAO's Evidence-Based Training framework, commercially deployed AI training tools, and the Part 141 modernization initiative — creates a plausible pathway for AI integration into FAA-credited avionics transition training within the next decade.

**ICAO's CBTA/EBT framework is inherently AI-compatible.** ICAO Doc 9868 (PANS-Training, Third Edition 2020) and Doc 9995 (Manual of Evidence-Based Training, 2013) establish competency-based training methodology that emphasizes demonstrated proficiency over fixed training hours. The nine ICAO pilot competencies — including Aircraft Flight Path Management (Automation), Situational Awareness, and Workload Management — map naturally to AI-measurable behavioral indicators. IATA's EBT Implementation Guide (Edition 2, January 2024), co-branded with ICAO and IFALPA, represents the current industry consensus. **IATA surveys show 47% of operators have implemented CBTA**, with 65% of non-implementers planning adoption within three years. AI is the critical enabler for scaling these data-intensive approaches.

EASA is establishing the global regulatory baseline. **NPA 2025-07** (November 2025) is the first regulatory proposal explicitly addressing AI trustworthiness in aviation, covering a Human-AI teaming framework. A second NPA expected in 2026 will deploy this framework across aviation domains including training. CAE has already begun CBTA rollout for business aviation under EASA, starting January 2026. The risk for the FAA is that European standards will define the global approach without U.S. input on training-specific provisions.

The **FAA Part 141 modernization initiative** (Docket FAA-2024-2531), with public meetings held in January 2026, offers the most timely opportunity. Industry comments from the University Aviation Association and NATA explicitly requested "expanded use of FSTDs and adoption of Virtual/Augmented Reality Training" and "integration of Scenario and Competency Based Training." The **Human Factors ARC**, chartered by the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 (P.L. 118-63) and holding its first plenary meeting on January 15–16, 2025, has expertise in human-machine integration relevant to AI training systems. Section 801 of the Reauthorization Act also created the Office of Advanced Aviation Technology and Innovation and its Steering Committee.

Military precedents demonstrate feasibility. The U.S. Army's Intelligent Flight Trainer at Fort Rucker pioneered adaptive coaching for helicopter pilots. The USAF's 2023 prototype adaptive VR training for F-16 cockpit procedures adjusted difficulty based on cognitive workload. FlightSafety's FlightSmart is deployed under contract with USAF Air Education and Training Command. USC's Institute for Creative Technologies developed Mission PAL using generative AI for adaptive military training content generation with statistically significant learning gains.

**Human factors risks demand careful management.** Mode confusion remains the dominant challenge in avionics transitions — a 1999 Australian survey found **73% of pilots had inadvertently selected wrong modes** and 61% agreed automation still surprised them. NASA's CRM-A framework (Shively et al., 2018) proposes treating advanced automation as a team member requiring bi-directional communication, shared mental models, and working agreements. AI-driven training could either mitigate or exacerbate automation dependency: AI can detect dependency patterns and introduce targeted manual-flying scenarios, but overly optimized training could paradoxically reinforce technology over-reliance. The FAA's PARC/CAST working group found insufficient crew knowledge of automated systems was a factor in **more than one-third of accidents and serious incidents**.

## Conclusion

The regulatory and industry landscape for avionics transition training stands at an inflection point. The FAA's mature but static framework — FSB reports, five-tier differences levels, deterministic FSTD qualification — was designed for a world where avionics changes were incremental and cockpit interfaces evolved slowly. Honeywell Anthem's cloud-native, touch-first, AI-assisted architecture represents a fundamentally different training challenge that existing regulations barely contemplate.

Three actionable insights emerge. First, the **SFAR pathway that introduced AQP should serve as the model** for a business aviation AI training innovation program — a controlled, data-driven regulatory sandbox allowing Part 91/135 operators to demonstrate equivalent or better safety outcomes with AI-adaptive training tools. Second, **Part 60's deterministic FSTD qualification paradigm needs an addendum** addressing AI-generated scenario elements, distinguishing between the validated aircraft performance model (which must remain deterministic) and the training scenario layer (which could be adaptive without compromising simulation fidelity). Third, **Epic 3.0 as a bridge product creates a natural regulatory on-ramp** — the training differences between Epic and Epic 3.0 will be modest enough for Level B–C classification, while establishing pilot familiarity with touch interfaces, connected workflows, and AI-assisted features that will define the full Anthem experience.

The organizations best positioned to drive this evolution are those already straddling the regulatory and technology boundaries: FlightSafety with FlightSmart AI and VITAL MR, CAE with its CBTA rollout and adaptive learning engine, and Honeywell itself as both an avionics OEM and training ecosystem architect. The 10-year window aligns with Anthem's likely entry into mainstream business aviation via the Bombardier partnership, EASA's AI framework maturation, and the FAA's Part 141 modernization. Whether the FAA leads or follows on AI-driven training will depend on whether the current rulemaking initiatives — the Human Factors ARC, the Part 141 review, and the draft AC 120-53C — incorporate explicit provisions for adaptive, technology-enhanced training or default to codifying existing practice.
