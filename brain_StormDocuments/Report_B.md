# Automated pilot evaluation through AI-driven ATC voice analysis

**AI-based assessment of pilot-ATC communications is technically feasible today and rapidly approaching operational deployment, but remains constrained by accent-driven speech recognition errors, absent regulatory frameworks, and the non-negotiable requirement for human instructor authority.** The convergence of mature speech recognition systems (achieving **3–5% word error rates** for controller speech), validated cognitive load indicators extractable from voice data, and ICAO's push toward competency-based training creates a compelling—but incomplete—pathway for integrating automated grading into Honeywell Anthem training environments. DLR's aviation speech recognition technology reached **TRL6 in 2024** and was licensed commercially, while systems like CAE Rise and FlightSafety's FlightSmart already deploy objective data analytics in simulator training. Yet no regulator has published specific guidance for AI-based pilot assessment, and peer-reviewed validation comparing AI to human grading remains conspicuously absent from the literature.

---

## Quantifiable KPIs that voice interactions can yield

The richest source of objective pilot performance data lies in the communication exchange itself. Research from the FAA, DLR, and the SESAR-funded HAAWAII project has validated a suite of extractable metrics that map directly to training competencies.

**Readback accuracy** is the most mature metric. FAA research (Prinzo, Hendrix & Hendrix, 2006) analyzing 50 hours of TRACON communications found that readback errors increase sharply with message complexity—messages containing four aviation topics produced error rates of **0.30**, while two-topic messages dropped to **0.062**. Only **1.74% of omission errors** were self-corrected. The HAAWAII project found approximately **1.3% of total utterances** contain readback errors in live operations, with substitution errors (wrong altitude, heading, or frequency values) the most common and dangerous category. Modern Named Entity Recognition systems from the ATCO2 pipeline can automatically extract callsigns, command types, and numeric values, enabling real-time comparison between ATC instruction content and pilot readback content.

**Response latency**—the interval between ATC transmission end and pilot transmission start—provides a direct, objective workload proxy. FAA research established that delays beyond **400 milliseconds** in ATC operations become problematic, while the HAAWAII system flags a "MISSING_READBACK" state if no pilot response arrives within **30 seconds**. This metric requires only push-to-talk timing data and is trivially automatable.

**Phraseology compliance** can be scored against ICAO standard templates using NLU parsing. Common deviations include use of "point" instead of "decimal" for frequencies, omission of callsigns, and dropped altitude units. The ATCO2 corpus provides NER annotation classes (Callsign, Command, Value, Unnamed Phrase) that serve as the parsing backbone. HAAWAII achieved **97% callsign recognition** for controllers when integrating radar data, and a **92.5% command recognition rate** with only 2.4% command error rate across validation studies.

Additional automatable KPIs include clarification request frequency (FAA found **33% of foreign-registry communication problems** were requests for repetition versus 50% for native speakers), self-correction rate, communication duration, and transmission efficiency (number of exchanges per clearance). Collectively, these metrics form a composite communication performance score with established baselines against which individual pilot performance can be benchmarked.

## Cognitive load leaves measurable traces in the voice signal

Voice-based cognitive workload measurement has moved from laboratory curiosity to validated aviation application. The evidence base, while requiring per-speaker calibration, is robust enough for training system integration.

**Fundamental frequency (F0)** is the strongest single indicator. Huttunen et al. (2011) recorded 13 military pilots during simulator flights and found mean F0 increased by **7 Hz on average** under cognitive load, rising to **12 Hz** during the most intensive flight phases. Vocal intensity increased by **1–1.5 dB**, while F0 range narrowed by **5 Hz**—pilots literally speak in a higher, louder, more monotone voice when cognitively saturated. A NASA Ames study (Bittner, Begault & Christopher, 2013) confirmed significantly higher F0 during high-workload ATC readback tasks, though crucially found **no correlation between subjective workload ratings and acoustic measures across individuals**, underscoring that within-speaker baseline calibration is essential.

**Speech disfluencies** provide complementary evidence. Normal speech contains approximately **6 disfluencies per 100 words** (Fox Tree, 1995), with filled pauses ("um," "uh") comprising one-third to one-half of all disfluencies. Under increased cognitive load, disfluency rates rise, silent pauses lengthen, and disfluency clustering intensifies. Goldman-Eisler's foundational work established that nearly half of speaking time consists of pausing and disfluencies—a ratio that shifts upward under workload. Critically, "um" signals longer cognitive delays than "uh" (Clark & Fox Tree, 2002), offering granular load discrimination.

**Speech rate changes** are directionally complex. ICAO recommends **100 words per minute** for radiotelephony. While most studies report decreased articulation rate under cognitive load (Bittner et al., 2013; Springer, 2018), some find accelerated speech under stress (Johnstone & Scherer, 2004). The resolution lies in distinguishing speech rate (including pauses) from articulation rate (excluding pauses)—the former typically decreases while the latter shows more variable responses.

The most advanced integration study comes from Magnusdottir et al. (2022), who tested combined cardiovascular and voice measures on **97 university participants and 20 airline pilots**, achieving misclassification rates of **15.17%** and **17.38%** respectively for trinary workload classification (low/medium/high). A key finding: individual differences remain the primary limiting factor for generic classification, reinforcing the need for personalized baselines. For the Anthem training environment, this means establishing each pilot's voice profile during low-workload phases and tracking deviations throughout the session.

## Aviation speech recognition has reached a critical inflection point

The technology landscape for aviation STT/NLP has transformed dramatically since 2018, driven by foundation models and the maturation of DLR's assistant-based architecture. However, pilot speech recognition remains substantially harder than controller speech recognition—a gap that directly impacts training system design.

**The HAAWAII project** represents the state of the art. This SESAR-funded initiative (DLR, NATS, Isavia, Idiap) developed an end-to-end architecture combining voice activity detection, deep neural network ASR, ontology-based text-to-concept extraction, and surveillance data integration. Validated on 500+ hours of real ATC recordings from London and Icelandic airspace, HAAWAII achieved **<5% WER for controllers and <10% for pilots**, with **97% callsign recognition** when combining voice with radar data. The PROSA validation (September–November 2022) with 12 Austro Control controllers demonstrated **3% average WER** (range 0.7–8.2%), **92% command recognition rate**, and reduced controller mouse-clicking time by a factor of 30. Critically, the system produced a net safety improvement: radar label errors dropped from **11% to 4%** when ASRU was active—meaning even imperfect recognition improved safety over manual operations alone.

**MALORCA** proved that expensive manual adaptation could be replaced by machine learning. Semi-supervised adaptation using untranscribed recordings plus radar data reduced command error rates from **7.9% to 0.6%** at Prague and from **18.9% to 3.2%** at Vienna, with less than four hours of transcribed data required.

**Foundation models are reshaping the field.** OpenAI's Whisper achieves approximately **95% WER** on raw ATC data—essentially useless—but after domain-specific fine-tuning, performance improves dramatically: Whisper Large v3 fine-tuned achieved **6.5% WER**, while Wav2Vec 2.0 fine-tuned on the ATCOSIM corpus reached **1.67% WER** for controllers. TU Delft's open-source Whisper-ATC models set new benchmarks on the ATCO2 and ATCOSIM test sets. The critical insight is that general-purpose ASR is completely inadequate for aviation; domain adaptation is non-negotiable.

**The accent problem remains the primary unsolved challenge.** Pilot speech is roughly twice as difficult to transcribe as controller speech (Airbus 2018 Challenge finding), primarily because pilot accents are unpredictable while controllers operate from known locations. Southeast Asian-accented ATC speech achieved only **9.82% WER** even with fine-tuned Whisper models. Current mitigation strategies include region-specific model adaptation, contextual biasing using active flight lists, surveillance data integration for hypothesis constraining, and accent-aware training data augmentation. The ATCO2 corpus (5,281 hours from 10+ airports) deliberately spans multiple accent regions. DLR's SELF-MADE-ATC project (launched June 2025) addresses automated model maintenance with privacy-preserving on-premises training—a critical consideration for handling sensitive voice data.

The commercial landscape reached a milestone in 2024 when DLR licensed its speech understanding technology to **EML Speech Technology GmbH**, marking the first known commercialization of aviation-specific ASRU. Adacel's ICE system is already a commercial training product with accent-tolerant speech recognition. EUROCAE Working Group 126 released a public-domain ontology for ATC command annotation in July 2025, and NASA and DLR are harmonizing their command annotation frameworks.

## Mapping automated data to the CBTA competency architecture

The ICAO/IATA competency framework provides a structured target for automated assessment outputs. The mapping between ATC interaction data and CBTA competencies is stronger than commonly appreciated, extending well beyond the obvious Communication (COM) competency.

The IATA framework defines **10 Observable Behaviors for Communication**, of which **9 are amenable to automated measurement** from ATC interaction data. OB 2.5 (active listening, demonstrated by correct readback accuracy), OB 2.9 (standard radiotelephone phraseology adherence), and OB 2.3 (clear, accurate, concise message conveyance) map directly to extractable voice metrics. Only OB 2.8 (non-verbal communication) falls outside automated capture scope. The behaviors were numbered in 2018 specifically "to facilitate CBTA training data collection and analysis"—a clear institutional signal toward automated processing.

Cross-competency mapping extends the value substantially. **Workload Management (WLM)** evidence comes from communication timing during high-workload phases, cognitive load voice indicators, and delayed or missed radio calls. **Situational Awareness (SAW)** manifests in response latency to traffic calls, accuracy of position reports, and anticipation of expected clearances. **Knowledge (KNO)** is evidenced by correct aviation terminology use and understanding of airspace procedures in communications. **Problem Solving and Decision Making (PSD)** surfaces during non-normal scenarios through emergency declarations, clarification requests, and alternative course of action communications.

The EASA/IATA grading structure provides a standardized output format across four levels: Level 0 (competent/not competent binary), Level 1 (numeric 1–5 competency grade), Level 2 (specific Observable Behavior records), and Level 3 (Threat and Error Management records). An automated system generating structured data at all four levels would integrate seamlessly with existing training management systems.

**ICAO Doc 9995** (Manual of Evidence-Based Training) establishes the philosophical and methodological foundation for data-driven training but does not explicitly address AI-based assessment. It mandates flight data analysis to tailor training programs and requires that any competency assessed below standard be linked to specific observable behaviors. The IATA EBT Implementation Guide (Edition 2, January 2024) acknowledges that "EBT as any CBTA program generates a significant volume of training data" and recommends implementing standardized competency frameworks "to facilitate the analysis and benchmarking of the training and safety data."

The regulatory pathway is clearest through **EASA's AI Concept Paper (Issue 2, 2023/2024)**, which explicitly identifies "Assessment of training performance" as **Use Case F.5.1** in the Training/FSTD domain. An automated ATC interaction assessment tool would classify as a **Level 1 AI application** ("assistance to human"), requiring compliance with four trustworthiness building blocks: Learning Assurance, AI Explainability, Ethics-based Assessment, and Safety Risk Mitigation. The FAA's **Advanced Qualification Program (AC 120-54A)** provides complementary latitude, explicitly encouraging "innovation in the methods and technology that are used during instruction and evaluation" and stating that "alternate means are encouraged and will be evaluated on their merit."

## The barriers are real but navigable

The path from technical feasibility to operational deployment crosses five distinct friction zones, each requiring deliberate mitigation.

**Regulatory uncertainty** is the most fundamental barrier. No aviation regulator has published specific guidance for AI-based training assessment tools. EASA's NPA 2025-07 is the most advanced proposal, covering Level 1 and Level 2 AI applications, but Rulemaking Task RMT.0742 will not complete until end of 2027. The FAA AI Safety Roadmap (July 2024) acknowledges that "AI evolves too quickly for prescriptive rules" and establishes principles rather than requirements. Software certification standards (DO-178C) do not address ML systems. This creates a window where innovative systems can be proposed under existing frameworks (particularly AQP) but face uncertain approval timelines.

**Instructor trust** requires transparent, explainable AI design. FlightSafety International's Chris Starr captured the core pilot concern: "Why is a computer grading me?" Every operational system in the industry—CAE Rise, FlightSmart, AXIS AI Debriefing, APC's PROJECT ORCA—maintains absolute instructor override authority. Halldale Group's editorial warning is instructive: "Technologies that cannot be explained, bounded, overridden and audited are unsuitable, regardless of sophistication." Research further shows that AI grading tends to be more lenient on low performers and harsher on high performers (Wetzler et al., 2024), reinforcing the case for human-in-the-loop design.

**Validation evidence** is the most conspicuous gap. No large-scale published study directly comparing AI grading to expert human grading with rigorous statistical methodology was identified in this research. NASA-funded work (Heath et al., 2005) found correlations between automated flight parameter metrics and CFI assessments for landing approaches, and DLR-associated studies on NOTECHS inter-rater reliability provide baseline human agreement rates (κ ranging from poor to excellent depending on rating dimension, with self-rating/instructor agreement averaging ~0.5). CAE validated its system with the Japan Air Self-Defense Force and Southern Methodist University, and FlightSafety's FlightSmart completed USAF acceptance testing on 16 T-6A devices. But these validations are proprietary rather than published in peer-reviewed literature. For an automated ATC grading system, the critical benchmark is demonstrating **AI-human agreement at or above human-human agreement** using standard IRR metrics (Cohen's κ, Fleiss' κ, or ICC), which EASA requires for concordance management.

**Accent-driven STT limitations** directly constrain grading accuracy. If the system cannot reliably transcribe what a pilot said, it cannot reliably evaluate whether what they said was correct. The current **8–10% pilot WER** means approximately 1 in 10 words is misrecognized, creating a floor below which grading accuracy cannot fall without additional error correction mechanisms. Contextual biasing (using scenario-specific expected phraseology), N-best hypothesis comparison, and confidence thresholds for flagging uncertain recognitions are essential architectural features.

**Cultural resistance** from pilot unions (ALPA, IFALPA, BALPA) centers on data privacy, punitive use potential, and job protection. IFALPA's ICAO Assembly working paper raised concerns about CBTA complexity and insufficient evidence of effectiveness—concerns that extend naturally to automated assessment. Transparent data governance, explicit prohibitions on punitive use, and GDPR-compliant data handling are prerequisites for acceptance.

## The Honeywell Anthem opportunity and the competitive landscape

Honeywell Anthem's architecture creates unique advantages for integrated AI-based training assessment. As the first **cloud-native, always-connected cockpit system**, Anthem's Integrated Network Server Unit provides native data offload capabilities, while the Honeywell Forge cloud platform enables fleet-wide analytics. The Connected Mission Manager already anticipates pilot actions based on flight context—the same contextual awareness could power expected-response models for ATC interaction assessment. The third-party secure cockpit browser enables hosting training and debriefing applications directly on the flight deck platform.

Anthem's current aircraft integration targets—primarily eVTOL platforms (Vertical Aerospace VX4, Lilium Jet, Supernal) and advanced general aviation—align with the Simplified Vehicle Operations concept that aims to reduce pilot training requirements. This creates a natural demand signal for automated competency verification as reduced crew operations and novel aircraft categories require more rigorous, data-driven evidence of pilot proficiency.

The competitive landscape is intensifying. **CAE Rise** leads in deployed capability, processing simulator telemetry and eye-tracking data across 240+ training centers with plans for AI to drive **20% of crew-level and 10% of individual pilot training content**. **FlightSafety's FlightSmart** monitors 4,000+ variables in real-time across USAF deployments. **APC's PROJECT ORCA** is the most ambitious new entrant, processing audio/video directly (bypassing STT entirely) to map pilot behaviors to ICAO competencies, with initial customer deployment planned for Q1 2026. **AXIS Flight Simulation** offers automated maneuver recognition and CBTA-mapped debriefing. All position themselves as instructor augmentation tools, none as autonomous graders—a unanimous industry consensus.

## Conclusion

Three findings from this research stand out as actionable for Anthem training system development. First, the **KPI extraction capability is mature**: readback accuracy, response latency, phraseology compliance, callsign usage, and cognitive load voice markers all have validated measurement methods with established baselines. The composite scoring framework effectively writes itself from existing research. Second, the **regulatory pathway exists but requires proactive engagement**: EASA's explicit identification of training performance assessment as an AI use case (F.5.1) and the FAA AQP's encouragement of innovative methods create openings, but the absence of specific guidance means early movers will shape the regulatory framework. Third, the **validation gap represents both risk and opportunity**: the first system to publish rigorous, peer-reviewed AI-versus-human grading concordance data will establish the credibility benchmark for the entire field. Given that human inter-rater reliability in CBTA averages only **~0.5–0.6** (and is described as "excessively complex" by practitioners), an AI system achieving even modest agreement levels could demonstrably improve assessment consistency—which may prove to be the strongest argument for adoption.

The technology is ready for deployment as an instructor decision-support tool. It is not ready—and regulators will not accept it—as an autonomous grading system. The winning architecture combines DLR-lineage assistant-based speech recognition with per-speaker cognitive load baselining, CBTA-structured output formatting, full instructor override authority, and transparent explainability for every assessment decision.
